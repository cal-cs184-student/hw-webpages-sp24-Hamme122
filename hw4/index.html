In this project, we learned how to create wireframes in order to simulate cloth falling. We did this by creating a system of pointers, masses, and springs, which we attached to each other and then moved around in order to create a cloth-like object. We also learned how to create simulations or animations, as well as how to deal with bugs like clipping, which we did through managing collisions, both with other objects as well as itself. This was created by using Hooke’s law to create forces to move the cloth, and also Verlet integration in order to compute the position of the point_mass at each timestep. The clipping and collisions were dealt with by checking for different constraints which we then used to compute the positions of the point_masses, moving them away from the surfaces they come in contact with, while keeping in mind friction. For self_collisions, we placed each point_mass in an imaginary 3D space and divided them up into a hashmap. Using the hashmap, we compared each of the point_masses to ones nearby and then recomputed their positions. Finally, we also learned how to shade and texture objects using Diffuse Shading, Blinn-Phong Shading, Texture Mapping, such as displacement mapping and bump mapping, and finally Environment-Mapped Reflections, creating them using multiple equations given in class.

  1. Change the spring constant ks

We can see that the change in ks will change the stiffness of the fabric. A high spring constant means the cloth is very stiff and resists deformation so the folds will be few while a low spring constant means the fabric is more flexible and easily deformed, resulting in more numerous and softer folds.

Additionally, when ks is low, the cloth will take a long time to settle into its final resting position due to the low restoring force provided by the low spring constant and during motion, it will sway and deform easily, responding strongly to even small external forces. This is opposite to the high ks.

  1. Change the density

Changing the density of the object affects the heaviness of the cloth, resulting in more noticeable sagging and slightly faster descent. A higher density causes the top of the cloth to appear highly curved, with the folds becoming more defined as external forces push the PointMasses farther downward. A lower density has the opposite effect on the cloth. As it descends, its movements appear more gradual and fluid, with less emphasis on the formation of deep folds.

  1. Change the damping

Adjusting the damping of the cloth alters its speed of position changes. Lower damping values lead to faster position changes. It is just like the cloth is flying. Conversely, with a big damping value, the cloth descends gradually and gracefully, as the rate of position change is diminished.

  1. Introduction of the shader program

A shader program is an algorithm that calculates how much amount of light and shadow certain parts of an object has and applies it to the parts. Shader programs are made up of vertex shaders and fragment shaders. We use vertex shaders to move vertices and change their vector, to create a final vertex position, which we then use as an input into fragment shaders, which uses it to compute a color and apply it to the material.

Below are the results using the texture_3.png

Below are the results using o 16 -a 16 and then o 128 -a 128.

Adjusting the sphere's roughness seems to have a bigger impact on displacement mapping compared to bump mapping. Displacement mapping has stronger shadows and bumpier textures compared to bump mapping. On the other hand, bump mapping is much smoother with fewer shadows appearing, especially near the edges.

In the o 16 -a 16 version, bump mapping seems more realistic than displacement mapping as the shape of the sphere is very uneven. Along with the way the shadows drape over the bumpy sphere it makes the object look even more unrealistic, as they don’t follow any of the shapes of the bumps, but rather trace the shape as if the sphere was completely round. As such the smoother bump-mapping sphere looks a lot closer to life, as the shadows actually follow the shape.

In the -o 128 -a 128 version, displacement mapping makes the sphere look a lot more realistic than if it were to use bump mapping. This is because the sphere is not completely smooth, having lots of texturization, and the ridges and the shadows follow this shape, being darker in the crevices. On the other hand, bump-mapping continues to produce a relatively smooth ball, with similar shadows to the one in theo 16 -a 16.
